Modern space technologies

Next-generation space launchers

Space launchers form the foundation of all modern space exploration activity. Since the first successful flight of Falcon 9 in 2010, SpaceX has revolutionized the aerospace industry by developing reusable rockets. The Falcon 9, 70 meters long and capable of carrying 22.8 tonnes to low Earth orbit, has completed over 200 successful launches to date. The company also launched the Falcon Heavy in February 2018, the most powerful launcher currently in service, capable of placing 63.8 tonnes in low Earth orbit.

NASA's Artemis program, initiated in 2017, represents an ambitious objective to return to the Moon. The Space Launch System (SLS), developed since 2011, is designed to become the most powerful launcher ever built, with a capacity of 130 tonnes in lunar orbit. The first integrated test of the SLS, named Artemis I, lifted off on November 16, 2022 without a crew, marking a crucial step toward future crewed flights.

  • Falcon 9: 70 meters tall, launch cost reduced to 60 million dollars
  • Falcon Heavy: 70 meters tall, payload capacity of 63.8 tonnes in low orbit
  • Space Launch System (SLS): 111 meters tall, payload capacity of 130 tonnes to the Moon
  • Ariane 6 (ESA): first launched in July 2024, capable of 11.5 tonnes in geostationary orbit
  • Long March 9 (China): in development, objective of 140 tonnes in low Earth orbit by 2030

Space telescopes and observing the universe

The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST), launched on December 25, 2021 after three decades of development and a cost of 10 billion dollars, has transformed our understanding of the early universe. With a primary mirror of 6.5 meters composed of 18 hexagonal beryllium segments covered in gold, the JWST observes primarily in the infrared. In July 2022, the telescope transmitted its first scientific images, revealing galaxies formed only 100 to 250 million years after the Big Bang.

The legacy of the Hubble Space Telescope, launched in 1990, remains impressive despite its advanced age. Hubble made it possible to precisely determine the rate of expansion of the universe, named the Hubble constant, and contributed to discovering the existence of dark energy. In 2024, Hubble continues to operate in low Earth orbit at 600 kilometers altitude, although some of its instruments have required regular repairs since its launch.

The ESA's Euclid Space Telescope, launched in July 2023, focuses on mapping the structures of dark matter and dark energy. With a scientific payload weighing 280 kilograms, Euclid observes approximately 10 billion galaxies to create the largest 3D map of the universe ever made.

Planetary exploration missions

NASA's Perseverance rover, which arrived on Mars on February 18, 2021, continues its exploration mission from Jezero Crater. Weighing 899 kilograms and equipped with 19 cameras, Perseverance searches for traces of past microbial life and collects rock samples for a future Mars sample return mission. As of March 2024, the rover has traveled more than 29 kilometers and collected 23 rock samples.

NASA's Juno probe, orbiting Jupiter since July 2016, has radically changed our knowledge of the gas giant. By descending to a minimum altitude of 350 kilometers above the Jovian clouds, Juno discovered that Jupiter's magnetic fields are far more complex than expected and that the planet's core could be less dense than anticipated.

China's space program has also made remarkable progress. The Zhurong rover landed on Mars on May 14, 2021 and transmitted valuable data about the Martian subsurface. More recently, the Chang'e 5 mission returned 1,731 grams of lunar samples in December 2020, marking the first time since 1976 that a space agency has returned lunar rocks to Earth.

Space stations and continuous human presence in orbit

The International Space Station, whose construction extended from 1998 to 2011, remains a symbol of international space cooperation. 109 meters long and 73 meters wide, the ISS orbits at 400 kilometers altitude with a mass of 420 tonnes. In 2024, the station regularly hosts a crew of seven astronauts, ensuring continuous human presence in low Earth orbit since November 2000.

China launched its own modular space station, Tiangong (The Celestial Palace), with the core module Tianhe launched in April 2021. In November 2022, the inaugural crew joined the station, marking the beginning of Chinese scientific operations in orbit. Tiangong extends 70 meters and can accommodate three astronauts permanently.

Advanced propulsion technologies

Liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen rocket engines, used since the 1960s, remain dominant for main launchers. However, new technologies are emerging. SpaceX's Merlin engine, running on kerosene and liquid oxygen, has been optimized for reusability and currently has over 200 successful launches. Each Merlin engine generates 190 tonnes of thrust at ground level.

Electric ionic propulsion systems are gaining importance for long-distance missions. The Deep Space 1 probe, launched in 1998, was the first to use this type of propulsion. Today, the NSTAR Xenon ion engine produces a specific impulse of 3,100 seconds, allowing fuel consumption well below traditional chemical engines. Future missions to Mars and beyond will likely use this technology. NASA is also considering thermal nuclear engines, which could reduce travel time to Mars from 6 months to just 3 months.

Megaconstellations of satellites and space connectivity

SpaceX has deployed over 5,400 Starlink satellites in low Earth orbit since 2019, with a final goal of 12,000 satellites. These constellations aim to provide global high-speed Internet connectivity. Each Starlink satellite weighs approximately 260 kilograms and orbits at 550 kilometers altitude in interconnected mesh formations.

Amazon is preparing to launch its Project Kuiper constellation of 3,236 satellites, while China is progressively deploying its Hongyan constellation. These systems represent a paradigm shift in the use of low Earth orbit, transforming space into critical communication infrastructure for the planet.

"Space is the final frontier. It is our destiny to explore the stars." - Wernher von Braun, pioneer of space exploration, 1966. This pioneering vision still guides modern space agencies in their ambitions for ever more distant exploration.
